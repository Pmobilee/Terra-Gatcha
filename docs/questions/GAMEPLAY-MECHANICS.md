# Gameplay & Mechanics — 50 Questions for Roadmap Expansion

> These questions explore mining mechanics, progression loops, economy balance,
> combat systems, and game feel. Answer them to refine Phases 7-25 of the roadmap.
> Each question references the current game state and includes professional advice.

---

## A. Mining Core & Layer Design (Questions 1-10)

### Q1: How should the 20-layer size scaling curve actually work?
**Context**: DD-V2-023 says layers scale from 20x20 (layer 1) to 40x40 (layer 20). The current implementation uses a flat 20x40 grid (`MINE_WIDTH: 20`, `MINE_LAYER_HEIGHT: 40`). The scaling formula is listed as "to be determined."
**My Advice**: A linear 20x20 to 40x40 over 20 layers feels too gradual — the difference between layer 5 (25x25) and layer 6 (26x26) would be imperceptible. I would recommend a stepped approach: layers 1-5 at 20x20, layers 6-10 at 25x25, layers 11-15 at 30x30, layers 16-20 at 40x40. Discrete jumps at tier boundaries feel more meaningful than smooth curves, and they naturally create "era" milestones the player can feel.

### Q2: Should layers have distinct gameplay identities beyond biome visuals?
**Context**: Right now each layer is a new biome with different block distributions, but the core loop is identical — mine blocks, find stuff, locate descent shaft. Over 20 layers this risks feeling repetitive regardless of visual variety.
**My Advice**: Consider giving every 5th layer a special structural identity — layer 5 could be a "gauntlet" (narrow corridors, dense hazards, rich rewards), layer 10 a "treasure vault" (open cavern full of nodes but timed by oxygen), layer 15 an "ancient archive" (artifact-heavy, quiz-heavy), layer 20 a "primordial core" (unique boss encounter or puzzle sequence). These break the pattern just enough to create anticipation for landmark layers.

### Q3: What happens when a player reaches layer 20 and completes it?
**Context**: The roadmap defines 20 as the max layer count but says nothing about what happens when a player clears the final layer. There is no endgame event, victory screen, or prestige system described.
**My Advice**: Layer 20 should be a memorable climax — a unique hand-crafted experience, not another procedural grid. Consider a "Core Discovery" event: a special artifact that reveals a major lore revelation about why Earth was abandoned, with GAIA delivering an emotional monologue. After that, unlock a prestige system — "New Game+" dives with modifier mutators (double hazards, half oxygen, rare-only minerals) that keep veterans engaged. The worst outcome is reaching the bottom and getting a "congratulations" toast.

### Q4: Should the player always start at a random position in each layer, or is top-entry better?
**Context**: DD-V2-022 says "players should be able to spawn everywhere in a layer." The current implementation always starts the player at the top center with a 3x3 cleared area. Random spawn was proposed to make each layer feel exploratory.
**My Advice**: Random spawning in later layers is good for replayability, but layer 1 should always start top-center — it is the most intuitive entry point and essential for onboarding. For layers 2-20, spawn the player near the top 25% of the layer but at a random horizontal position. Never spawn them in the bottom half — the descent shaft is down there, and spawning near it would trivialize exploration. A short "landing" animation from the descent shaft above grounds the spatial logic.

### Q5: How should block-type distribution change across 20 layers?
**Context**: Current density values are flat — `DENSITY_MINERAL_NODES: 18`, `DENSITY_ARTIFACT_NODES: 6`, etc. are the same for every layer. The only scaling is `LAYER_HARDNESS_SCALE: 1.3` which makes blocks take more hits. With 20 layers, deeper layers need to feel meaningfully different in composition, not just harder.
**My Advice**: Create a "composition curve" per block type. Dirt should dominate layers 1-3 then virtually disappear by layer 10. Hard Rock should be absent in layers 1-5, minor in 6-10, and dominant in 15-20. Mineral node density should peak around layers 8-12 (the "sweet spot" that keeps mid-game rewarding) then decrease slightly in deep layers while shifting to higher tiers. Artifact density should slowly increase with depth, peaking at layers 15-20. This makes depth feel qualitatively different, not just quantitatively harder.

### Q6: Should unbreakable blocks evolve or should new impassable block types appear?
**Context**: `DENSITY_UNBREAKABLE_RATIO: 0.03` is constant. Unbreakable blocks currently serve as maze walls. In a 40x40 deep layer, 3% unbreakable creates roughly 48 permanent obstacles — potentially creating frustrating dead-ends at larger scales.
**My Advice**: Reduce unbreakable density in deeper layers (where grids are larger) to prevent claustrophobic mazes. Instead, introduce "conditionally breakable" blocks — blocks that require specific pickaxe tiers, bomb usage, or companion abilities to break. This creates meaningful gating tied to player progression rather than hard walls. For example, a "Crystallized Wall" that only the Diamond Pick can scratch, or a "Sealed Chamber" that requires a bomb charge.

### Q7: How much should the descent shaft placement punish the player in deeper layers?
**Context**: DD-V2-024 says the descent shaft becomes harder to find in deeper layers. Currently `DESCENT_SHAFT_MIN_DEPTH_PERCENT: 0.5` places it in the bottom half. The shaft is always inside the quiz-gated sealed room (Phase 8.3).
**My Advice**: The shaft should be progressively harder to find but never unfairly hidden. I would recommend: layers 1-5 shaft in bottom 40%, layers 6-10 in bottom 30%, layers 11-15 in bottom 25%, layers 16-20 in bottom 15%. The scanner tier should meaningfully assist — a Deep Scanner (tier 4) should reveal the sealed room on the minimap when within 8 tiles. Without the scanner, the player relies on exploration and structural clues (ambient glow, unique wall patterns near sealed rooms). Never make it random-walk tedious — always give the attentive player signals.

### Q8: What is the ideal number of blocks mined per layer to feel "complete"?
**Context**: A 20x20 grid has 400 cells. With ~3% unbreakable, ~5 empty pockets, ~18 mineral nodes, etc., the player mines roughly 300-350 blocks to fully clear layer 1. A 40x40 grid would have ~1,400 mineable blocks. If a player is supposed to find the descent shaft and move on, how much of each layer should they typically explore?
**My Advice**: Target 40-60% exploration per layer for the "find the shaft and move on" path, and 80-95% for completionists. This means the sealed room should typically be findable after mining ~150 blocks in a 20x20 layer (40%) or ~600 blocks in a 40x40 layer (40%). Completionists who clear everything get bonus minerals but spend more oxygen. This ratio keeps sessions moving — a layer should take 2-4 minutes of active mining at the intended pace, meaning a full 20-layer dive is a 40-80 minute session (if a player reaches that deep, which most will not early on).

### Q9: Should there be horizontal movement between layers, or is every layer a self-contained grid?
**Context**: Currently each layer is a completely independent procedural grid. The player descends through a shaft and the previous layer is gone forever. There is no concept of going back up or moving laterally between connected spaces.
**My Advice**: Keep layers as self-contained grids — it is simpler to generate, simpler to reason about, and avoids the technical complexity of multi-layer persistence. However, consider a "Memory Echo" system: when entering a new layer, the minimap shows a ghosted overlay of the previous layer's explored area, giving a sense of geological continuity. The sealed room in each layer could occasionally have a "ventilation shaft" that reveals 1-2 tiles in the next layer before descending, giving a strategic preview. This creates the feeling of connected depth without the engineering cost.

### Q10: How should the 25 biomes map to the 20 layers?
**Context**: The roadmap defines 25 biomes (Phase 9) and 20 layers (Phase 8). Current implementation uses `generateBiomeSequence()` with Fisher-Yates shuffling across 3 biomes. With 25 biomes and 20 layers, some biomes will not appear in any given dive.
**My Advice**: Group biomes into depth tiers — Shallow (layers 1-5: Topsoil, Sedimentary, Sandstone, Coral Reef, Petrified Forest), Mid (layers 6-10: Volcanic, Granite, Fossilized, Fungal Forest, Salt Flats), Deep (layers 11-15: Crystalline, Crystal Caverns, Ancient City, Living Cave, Underground Ocean), Extreme (layers 16-20: Magma Core, Primordial, Meteorite Impact, Data Ruins, Geode Cathedral). Each dive randomly selects from the tier-appropriate pool. The remaining 5 biomes (Tar Pits, Toxic Waste, Volcanic Glass, Cloud Layer, Frozen) could be rare "anomaly" layers that override the normal tier with a 10-15% chance, creating surprise and variety.

## B. Combat, Hazards & Risk (Questions 11-20)

### Q11: Should hazards remain passive obstacles, or should some become active threats?
**Context**: Current hazards (Lava, Gas Pocket, Unstable Ground, Earthquakes) are all static or reactive — they only hurt you when you touch or mine them. There are no enemies, no active pursuers, no time pressure beyond oxygen. Phase 25.1 mentions a "Combat System" but places it post-launch.
**My Advice**: Before committing to full combat, consider "environmental predators" — hazards that move or expand over time. A lava flow that slowly fills adjacent cells every 30 seconds. A gas cloud that drifts toward the player. A crystal growth that seals passages if you linger. These create urgency without requiring a combat system, AI pathfinding, or attack animations. They turn the mine from a static puzzle into a dynamic environment where time matters beyond oxygen.

### Q12: What replaces the point-of-no-return as the primary tension mechanic?
**Context**: DD-V2-022 explicitly removes the point-of-no-return (`POINT_OF_NO_RETURN_PERCENT: 0.6` still in balance.ts but slated for removal). The player can always surface from any layer. Without it, the primary tension source is oxygen depletion — but oxygen is relatively generous (300 base, +15 per layer, caches throughout).
**My Advice**: Replace it with an "oxygen decay" curve — the deeper you go, the faster oxygen drains per action. Layer 1 costs are normal (1 per move, 1 per dirt). By layer 10, all costs are 1.5x. By layer 20, all costs are 2.5x. This creates natural pressure: you can always surface, but the deeper you are the more expensive it is to mine your way back to a send-up station or shaft. Combined with the "surface any time" freedom, this creates a constant risk calculation: "I can go deeper, but getting back will cost proportionally more oxygen." The tension is economic, not binary.

### Q13: How should the earthquake system scale across 20 layers?
**Context**: `EARTHQUAKE_CHANCE_PER_BLOCK: 0.008` (0.8%) with a cooldown of 15 blocks. Earthquakes collapse 8 blocks and reveal 4. In a 20x20 layer this is occasionally disruptive; in a 40x40 deep layer it is barely noticeable.
**My Advice**: Scale earthquake intensity with depth — deeper layers should have stronger and more frequent quakes. Layers 1-5: current values. Layers 6-10: 1.2% chance, 12 collapse, 6 reveal. Layers 11-15: 1.5% chance, 16 collapse, 8 reveal. Layers 16-20: 2% chance, 20 collapse, 10 reveal, and introduce "aftershocks" (50% chance of a second smaller quake within 5 blocks). This makes deep dives feel geologically unstable and creates real danger without adding enemies.

### Q14: Should the cave-in system have a way to protect yourself, or is it purely punitive?
**Context**: `CAVE_IN_COLLAPSE_CHANCE: 0.4` with radius 2. Unstable Ground appears at 30%+ depth. When triggered, surrounding blocks collapse randomly. There is no player agency — no dodge, no warning, no protection.
**My Advice**: Add a 0.5-second "rumble" warning (screen shake + dust particles) before the collapse resolves, giving players time to move 1 tile away. Additionally, the "Hard Hat" relic or a craftable "Support Beam" consumable could reduce collapse radius by 1. Players should feel they could have avoided the damage if they reacted faster or came prepared. Purely random punishment with no counterplay feels unfair; counterplay with a skill check feels exciting.

### Q15: Should bombs be expanded into a broader consumable tool system?
**Context**: Bombs are the only consumable tool (3x3 area, 5 O2 cost, max 3 stack). They are found in upgrade crates. The design doc mentions "flares" and "shields" as future consumables but nothing is specified.
**My Advice**: Yes — create 4-5 consumable tool types found in crates or craftable. Bombs (current, area clear). Flares (reveals a 5x5 area through fog for 10 seconds — great for finding the sealed room). Shield Charges (blocks next hazard damage, single-use). Drill Charges (mines a straight line of 5 blocks in one direction — tunneling tool). Sonar Pulse (reveals all special blocks within 7-tile radius on the minimap for 5 seconds). Each tool creates a distinct tactical decision. Limit total consumable carry to 5 slots across all types to force interesting choices.

### Q16: Is the lava hazard interesting enough, or does it need mechanics beyond "costs 15 O2"?
**Context**: `LAVA_OXYGEN_COST: 15` is the only lava mechanic — mine it, lose oxygen. It appears at 40%+ depth at 3% density. There is no visual warning beyond the block sprite, no spreading, no temperature mechanic.
**My Advice**: Lava should be more dynamic. Instead of just being expensive blocks, lava should flow — mining a lava block causes adjacent empty cells to fill with lava over 3 seconds (slow enough to run, fast enough to create urgency). This makes mining near lava a genuinely tense decision: you might open a path but also unleash a flow that blocks your retreat. A "Lava Suit" relic could halve lava damage. A "Coolant Bomb" consumable could solidify a 3x3 lava area into obsidian (mineable, but hard). This single change would make volcanic layers dramatically more engaging.

### Q17: Should gas pockets have area-of-effect or remain single-tile hazards?
**Context**: `GAS_POCKET_OXYGEN_DRAIN: 8` triggers when you step on a gas pocket cell. There is no lingering effect, no spread, no visual zone of danger.
**My Advice**: Gas pockets should release a visible cloud when triggered that fills a 3x3 area for 5 seconds. Standing in the cloud drains 2 O2/second. The cloud is visible (green mist particles) and dissipates over time. This makes gas pockets area-denial hazards that force the player to retreat and wait or find a route around. It also creates interesting interactions with bombs (detonating near gas creates a larger cloud) and with the ventilation relic (clears gas instantly). Single-tile hazards that you walk onto and forget are not scary; lingering area hazards that reshape the space around them are.

### Q18: How should companion effects interact with hazards?
**Context**: Companions have typed effects including `hazard_resist` (reduces hazard damage). 10 effect types are implemented. But the interaction is a flat damage reduction with no strategic depth.
**My Advice**: Each companion species should have a specific hazard affinity, not generic resistance. A fire-themed fossil (ancient salamander) could grant lava immunity but no gas protection. An ancient bird could detect gas pockets 3 tiles away (visual indicator on fog). A trilobite could sense earthquakes 5 seconds early (rumble warning plays sooner). This makes companion choice a strategic decision tied to which biome tier you expect to explore. "Which pet do I bring?" becomes a meaningful pre-dive question.

### Q19: Should there be a "danger level" indicator that increases as you linger in a layer?
**Context**: Currently there is no time pressure within a layer beyond oxygen. A player with ample oxygen can methodically clear every single block with zero escalating risk. This removes urgency and tension.
**My Advice**: Implement a subtle "instability" meter per layer that increases over time (or blocks mined). At 50% instability: minor visual cues (dust falls from ceiling, small screen rumbles). At 75%: earthquake frequency doubles. At 100%: a "collapse warning" gives the player 30 seconds to reach the descent shaft or a send-up station before a catastrophic collapse that forces emergency surface with 50% loot loss. This creates a natural session timer per layer without hard-gating content, and it rewards efficient exploration over exhaustive clearing.

### Q20: How should difficulty scale for players who repeatedly dive to the same shallow layers?
**Context**: A cautious player could grind layers 1-3 endlessly for safe, low-risk mineral farming. Nothing prevents this or makes it less efficient over time. The only carrot for going deeper is rarer mineral tiers (shards at 40% depth, etc.).
**My Advice**: Implement "mineral depletion" — shallow layers that have been mined many times yield progressively fewer minerals (10% reduction per repeat dive to the same layer range, minimum 30% of base). Display this as "This depth has been over-mined. GAIA suggests going deeper." This gently pushes players toward progression without punishing them harshly. Simultaneously, ensure that layers 4-6 are clearly more rewarding than grinding 1-3, creating a natural "pull" downward. The stick is mild depletion; the carrot is exponentially better loot at depth.

## C. Progression Loops & Economy (Questions 21-30)

### Q21: Is the 110:1 mineral conversion ratio too punitive?
**Context**: `MINERAL_CONVERSION_RATIO: 100` with `MINERAL_CONVERSION_TAX: 0.10` means 110 dust = 1 shard. The economy doc says conversion exists as a sink, but 110:1 means a player needs ~12,100 dust to get 1 crystal (110 dust per shard, 110 shards per crystal). At 5-15 dust per mineral node with ~18 nodes per layer, that is hundreds of dives for a single crystal via conversion alone.
**My Advice**: The conversion should exist as an emergency option, not a primary path. But 110:1 makes it essentially useless. Consider 50:1 with no tax (or 100:1 with no tax, removing the extra 10%). The real question is whether conversion should be viable at all or purely a dust sink. If you want it viable, 30:1 or 50:1. If it is purely ceremonial, keep 100:1 but make that explicit in the UI ("This is inefficient. Finding higher-tier minerals in deeper layers is recommended.").

### Q22: How should crafting costs scale to prevent mid-game inflation?
**Context**: `SCALING_CRAFT_MULTIPLIER: 1.25` increases each repeat craft by 25%. 10 standard recipes exist. The economy doc warns about veteran players accumulating infinite minerals with nothing to spend them on.
**My Advice**: 1.25x scaling on repeat crafts is a good start but needs a cap — after 10 crafts of the same recipe, costs become absurd (1.25^10 = 9.3x base cost). Cap scaling at 3x base cost. More importantly, add "maintenance" costs to permanent upgrades — pickaxe tiers degrade slowly (every 50 dives, lose 10% efficiency until repaired). Repair costs scale with tier. This creates a steady drain that veteran players must engage with. Combined with the 2% dust decay above 500 (`MINERAL_DECAY_RATE`), this prevents hoarding.

### Q23: Should there be a "dust per minute" target for each layer tier?
**Context**: No explicit income target exists. A player mining layer 1 (18 mineral nodes at 5-15 dust each = 90-270 dust per layer) earns roughly the same dust rate as any other layer, since deeper layers have harder blocks but no more mineral nodes. The only improvement is higher-tier mineral drops at depth.
**My Advice**: Define explicit income targets. Layer 1-5: ~50 dust/minute. Layer 6-10: ~80 dust/minute. Layer 11-15: ~120 dust/minute. Layer 16-20: ~200 dust/minute. Achieve this by increasing mineral node density and drop amounts at depth: `DENSITY_MINERAL_NODES` should be 18 at layer 1, scaling to 30 at layer 20. Drop ranges should increase too. This makes depth mathematically rewarding and gives you concrete numbers to balance crafting costs against.

### Q24: Are 10 standard recipes and 10 premium recipes enough for long-term engagement?
**Context**: The current crafting system has 10 standard recipes and 10 premium recipes. The economy doc lists categories (mining gear, scanner upgrades, backpack mods, O2 upgrades, base structures, consumables, cosmetics, pet revival) but specific recipe counts per category are not documented.
**My Advice**: 10 is enough for launch but will feel exhausted within a week of play. Plan for 30+ standard recipes across all categories by Phase 17 (Addictiveness Pass). More importantly, consider a "recipe discovery" system — players find recipe fragments in the mine (similar to Data Discs) that unlock new crafting options. This makes crafting progression feel exploratory rather than just menu-driven. A player who discovers a rare recipe feels ownership over that craft in a way that a static menu never provides.

### Q25: How should the rescue beacon interact with the 20-layer system?
**Context**: The economy doc defines the Rescue Beacon as a craftable consumable that prevents loot loss on oxygen depletion. Cost: 500 dust + 20 shards. It can also be found very rarely during dives.
**My Advice**: The beacon needs rebalancing for 20 layers. At layer 15 with a full backpack of crystals and geodes, the stakes are enormous — losing everything to oxygen depletion would be devastating. The current flat cost (500 dust + 20 shards) feels right for layers 1-5 but trivially cheap for the protection it provides at depth. Consider tiered beacons: Shallow Beacon (layers 1-7, 500 dust + 20 shards), Deep Beacon (layers 8-14, 2000 dust + 50 shards + 5 crystals), Abyssal Beacon (layers 15-20, 5000 dust + 100 shards + 20 crystals + 2 geodes). Alternatively, let one beacon work at any depth but make it consume more resources the deeper the extraction point is.

### Q26: Is the dive insurance system (`INSURANCE_COST_PERCENT: 0.15`) compelling or just a tax?
**Context**: Players can insure a dive for 15% of their dust holdings. Insurance keeps 100% of items on failure. This was added as an economy sink. The current implementation is a toggle in BaseView.
**My Advice**: 15% of holdings is regressive — it punishes wealthy players more in absolute terms but is the same percentage regardless of risk. A better model: insurance costs scale with which layer you intend to reach. Insuring a dive to layer 3 costs 5% of holdings. Insuring to layer 10 costs 15%. Insuring to layer 20 costs 30%. This makes insurance a genuine risk/reward calculation: "How deep do I plan to go, and is the protection worth the upfront cost?" Also, let players see the break-even point: "You need to find at least X minerals to profit after insurance." Make the math visible.

### Q27: Should the oxygen system move from "tanks per day" to "oxygen as a regenerating resource"?
**Context**: The game design says `STARTING_OXYGEN_TANKS: 3` per day. Each tank = 100 oxygen. The design doc mentions "oxygen refills over time" for free players and unlimited oxygen for subscribers. But the current implementation does not regenerate tanks — they are a flat daily allotment.
**My Advice**: Implement oxygen regeneration: 1 tank every 4 hours (6 tanks/day for a player who checks in 3 times). This is more mobile-friendly than a flat daily reset — it rewards players who do 2-3 short sessions over one long session. Subscribers get instant full tanks. Quiz mastery bonus tanks stack on top of regeneration. This model is proven (Duolingo hearts, many mobile F2P games). The key tuning question is the regeneration rate — too fast and there is no scarcity, too slow and free players feel punished. 4 hours per tank with a max of 6 stored tanks feels right for 5-15 minute sessions.

### Q28: How should the Knowledge Store powerups actually affect gameplay?
**Context**: BUG-002 notes that Knowledge Store powerups are defined but their effects are not wired into any game system. 10 items exist (4 powerups, 4 category boosts, 2 cosmetics). Knowledge Points = totalFactsLearned + masteredCount*2 + floor(quizCorrect/10).
**My Advice**: Powerups must have clearly felt, immediately visible effects. Suggested wiring: "Miner's Instinct" reveals all mineral nodes on the minimap for the next 3 dives. "Scholar's Focus" doubles quiz reward dust for the next 5 quizzes. "Deep Breath" adds +50 base oxygen permanently. "Lucky Strike" increases artifact rarity by one tier 20% of the time for the next 10 artifacts found. Make them powerful enough that earning Knowledge Points feels exciting, but limited enough (duration or charge-based) that players keep wanting more. Permanent unlocks are fine for 1-2 items; the rest should be renewable.

### Q29: What is the target session length, and does the current oxygen budget support it?
**Context**: The design doc says "Casual players (daily 5-minute dives)." With 300 oxygen (3 tanks at 100 each), mining dirt at 1 O2 per block, the player can mine 300 dirt blocks. At roughly 1 block per second (tap, mine, collect), that is 5 minutes. But harder blocks cost 2-5 O2, movement costs 1 O2, and hazards drain 8-15 O2. A realistic dive might last 2-4 minutes before oxygen runs out if the player is aggressive.
**My Advice**: Run a simulation. Count: average blocks mined before oxygen depletion at each layer tier, factoring in movement, block hardness distribution, oxygen caches (4 per layer at 30 O2 each = 120 bonus), and layer descent bonus (+15). For a target of 5-minute sessions reaching layers 2-3, the current budget might be tight. I suspect casual players will consistently run out of oxygen on layer 2, which is frustrating. Consider increasing `OXYGEN_PER_TANK` to 120 or `OXYGEN_CACHE_RESTORE` to 40 to give a comfortable 5-7 minute session through 2-3 layers.

### Q30: Should mineral tiers have gameplay effects beyond being currency denominations?
**Context**: The 5-tier mineral system (dust/shard/crystal/geode/essence) is purely economic — higher tiers are worth more, found deeper. They have no gameplay utility beyond spending at the Materializer and conversion between tiers.
**My Advice**: Give higher-tier minerals secondary uses that create interesting choices. Crystals could power a "Crystal Lantern" that expands fog reveal radius by 1 for 30 blocks (consumable use in-run). Geodes could be "cracked" in the mine to reveal a random relic effect for 1 layer. Essence could fuel a "Time Freeze" that pauses the instability meter for 60 seconds. This creates a dilemma: spend your crystal at the Materializer for a permanent upgrade, or use it in-run for an immediate tactical advantage? Dilemmas make currencies interesting.

## D. Tools, Relics & Companions (Questions 31-40)

### Q31: Is the pickaxe damage curve satisfying across 20 layers?
**Context**: Five pickaxe tiers: Stone(1), Iron(2), Steel(3), Diamond(5), Plasma(8). Block hardness: Dirt(1), Soft Rock(2), Stone(3), Hard Rock(5). With Stone Pick, hard rock takes 5 taps. With Plasma Cutter, dirt through hard rock are all 1-tap. The progression is front-loaded — the jump from Stone to Iron halves soft rock mining, but Diamond to Plasma barely matters since Diamond already handles everything in 1-2 hits.
**My Advice**: Introduce 2-3 new block types in deeper layers with hardness values of 8, 12, and 20. "Obsidian" (hardness 8) appears in layers 10+. "Primordial Stone" (hardness 12) in layers 15+. "Core Crystal" (hardness 20) in layers 18-20, only breakable with Plasma Cutter over multiple swings. This ensures every pickaxe tier has a layer range where it feels impactful, and the Plasma Cutter feels earned rather than overpowered. Without harder blocks, the top 2 pickaxe tiers are functionally identical.

### Q32: How should pickaxe upgrades work — permanent progression, or in-run only?
**Context**: Current system has both: permanent pickaxe tiers (from crafting, stored on PlayerSave) and in-run upgrade crates that boost damage by +1 for the current dive. The design doc says "in-run upgrades are the main power source" but permanent tiers are the current implementation.
**My Advice**: Permanent tiers should set your floor; in-run upgrades push you above it temporarily. A player with a permanent Steel Pick (damage 3) finds an in-run upgrade crate and gets +1 damage for this dive (effectively 4). This stacks up to +3 from crates (effectively 6 = between Diamond and Plasma). The permanent tier determines your baseline comfort; in-run upgrades create the "this is a great run" feeling that roguelites thrive on. Never let in-run upgrades exceed the next permanent tier by more than 1 — the permanent tiers must feel like meaningful milestones.

### Q33: Should the scanner system reveal more than just block types?
**Context**: Four scanner tiers reveal progressively more: radius 1-4, with higher tiers showing rarity and hazards. Current scanner functionality is fog-of-war penetration and colored hint dots.
**My Advice**: The Deep Scanner (tier 4) should unlock qualitative information, not just quantitative. At tier 4, hovering over a fog-of-war block should show a tooltip: "Contains: Mineral Node (Shard)" or "Hazard: Lava" or "Structure: Sealed Room nearby." This transforms the scanner from "I can see further" to "I can make informed decisions about where to mine." It also makes the scanner upgrade feel dramatically different from the pickaxe upgrade (which is about hitting harder). Each upgrade path should fulfill a distinct fantasy — the scanner is about knowledge, the pickaxe is about power.

### Q34: What is the right number of relic slots and how many total relics should exist?
**Context**: Currently 6 passive relics exist with 4 synergy combinations. Relics are found at Relic Shrines (2 per layer). The system has no slot limit documented — it appears players accumulate relics permanently.
**My Advice**: Cap active relic slots at 3 (with a slot expansion available via crafting or deep-dive rewards, up to max 5). This forces choosing which relics to equip before each dive. With 6 total relics and 3 slots, there are 20 possible loadouts — but with the 4 synergies, some combos are strictly better. Expand to 15-20 relics to create genuine build diversity. Group them into archetypes: "Explorer" relics (fog, movement, detection), "Miner" relics (damage, speed, resources), "Scholar" relics (quiz bonuses, fact quality, knowledge points), "Survivor" relics (oxygen, hazard resist, emergency). With 15 relics and 3 slots, you get 455 combinations — real replayability.

### Q35: Should relics have rarity tiers, or should all relics be equally weighted?
**Context**: Current relics are all equal — finding a shrine gives you a random relic with no rarity distinction. The gacha philosophy of the game (artifact rarities from common to mythic) is not applied to relics.
**My Advice**: Add 3 rarity tiers to relics: Common (found at any Relic Shrine), Rare (found only at shrines in layers 8+, or from the quiz-sealed room), and Legendary (one per relic type, found only in layers 15+ or crafted from premium materials). Common relics give +10-20% bonuses. Rare relics give +30-50% bonuses with an additional unique effect. Legendary relics fundamentally change how a system works (e.g., Legendary Scanner reveals the entire layer minimap on entry). This adds a gacha layer to relics and creates "chase items" that motivate deep dives.

### Q36: How should companion effects scale to stay relevant in deep layers?
**Context**: 10 companion effect types exist (mineral_rate, max_oxygen, instant_break, hazard_resist, etc.). Effects are flat bonuses. A companion that gives +10 max oxygen is meaningful at layer 1 (300 base to 310) but irrelevant at layer 15 where the player might have 500+ oxygen from upgrades and caches.
**My Advice**: Companion effects should be percentage-based, not flat: +5% max oxygen, +10% mineral drop quantity, +15% hazard resistance. Percentages scale naturally with the player's progression. Additionally, companions should "level up" through usage — a companion taken on 10 dives gains a minor secondary effect. Taken on 50 dives, it evolves visually and gains a stronger version of its primary effect. This creates attachment to specific companions and rewards loyalty over constant switching.

### Q37: Should there be a "tool belt" system for equipping consumables before a dive?
**Context**: Currently bombs are the only consumable, obtained during dives. The economy doc mentions "pre-run items (single-use bombs, flares, shields)" as craftable, but there is no pre-dive loadout screen.
**My Advice**: Add a pre-dive "Preparation" screen with 3 consumable slots. Players can equip crafted items before entering the mine. This creates a satisfying pre-dive ritual: choose your pickaxe tier (automatic, based on permanent upgrade), select your companion, equip 3 consumables (e.g., 2 bombs + 1 flare), review your relic loadout, then dive. The preparation screen also serves as a natural showcase for the crafting system — "I don't have a Shield Charge... I should craft one before my next deep dive." It turns crafting from abstract to immediately purposeful.

### Q38: How should the backpack system evolve for 20-layer dives?
**Context**: `STARTING_INVENTORY_SLOTS: 6` with up to 3 temporary expansions (+2, +2, +4 = 14 max). Stack sizes: dust(50), shard(20), crystal(5), geode(3), essence(1). A 20-layer dive with 18 mineral nodes per layer could yield over 360 mineral pickups across the full dive.
**My Advice**: 14 slots is too few for a 20-layer dive. The send-up station (1 per layer, 3 items max) helps but does not solve the problem — players will be constantly full and forced to discard. Two options: (a) Increase permanent backpack upgrades to 10 base with +4 per expansion (max 22), or (b) add an "auto-compress" upgrade that automatically converts 50 dust to 1 shard in the backpack, saving slots. Option (b) is more interesting because it creates a gameplay decision point (when to compress) and ties into the economy. The backpack should feel like a "how do I optimize this?" puzzle, not a "why is this always full?" frustration.

### Q39: Should the send-up station be more interactive than "select 3 items and send"?
**Context**: `SEND_UP_STATION_COUNT: 1` per layer (except last), `SEND_UP_MAX_ITEMS: 3`. The current flow is: arrive at station, select up to 3 items, they are secured. Simple and functional.
**My Advice**: The station is fine as-is mechanically, but consider two enhancements. First, the station should show what you have already sent up across all layers ("Previously sent: 45 dust, 3 shards, 1 artifact"). This reassures the player and reduces loss anxiety. Second, add a "rush delivery" option that costs 5 O2 but secures 5 items instead of 3 — creating an oxygen-vs-inventory tradeoff. Third, a very rare "Express Station" (replaces the normal station in ~20% of layers) that has no item limit but costs 10 O2 to use. Small variations on a simple system keep it interesting without overcomplicating it.

### Q40: Should there be a "sacrifice" mechanic for destroying items for immediate in-run benefits?
**Context**: BUG-007 notes a `sacrifice` screen type exists in the gameState type definition but no component was ever built. The concept was apparently planned but abandoned.
**My Advice**: Revive the concept but rename it to "Offering" (less dark, more thematic). At specific "Ancient Altar" structures in the mine (1 per layer at depth 50%+), the player can sacrifice minerals or artifacts for immediate benefits: 50 dust = +25 oxygen, 1 shard = reveal nearest 5 special blocks on minimap, 1 artifact = random relic effect for 1 layer, 1 crystal = full heal (oxygen to max). This creates a fascinating decision: the minerals you sacrifice are gone permanently (not just from this run), but the immediate benefit might save a failing dive. High-risk, high-reward — perfectly in line with roguelite philosophy.

## E. Game Feel, Quiz Integration & Session Design (Questions 41-50)

### Q41: How many taps should the most satisfying mining interaction take?
**Context**: DD-V2-012B ("The Pickaxe Feel") mandates an ultra-satisfying mining loop with wind-up, impact, and recovery frames. Currently, dirt is 1 tap and hard rock is 5 taps with the base pickaxe. The sweet spot for "satisfying repeated action" in mobile games is typically 2-4 taps.
**My Advice**: The most satisfying block to mine should take 3 taps — enough for the "progressive crack" feedback (hairline, major fracture, shatter) but not so many that it feels tedious. This means the "default" block at each layer tier should take 3 hits with the appropriate pickaxe tier. Dirt at 1 tap is fine (quick, disposable), but the rewarding blocks (mineral nodes, artifact nodes) should always take 3 taps regardless of pickaxe tier (with the damage showing progressively better crack animations). This preserves the satisfaction rhythm while letting pickaxe upgrades speed up traversal blocks.

### Q42: Should quiz difficulty scale with layer depth?
**Context**: Current quizzes draw from the fact pool with no difficulty weighting based on depth. A layer 1 quiz and a layer 20 quiz could ask the same question. The only scaling is quiz frequency (pop quiz rate, gate quizzes).
**My Advice**: Yes — layer depth should influence quiz difficulty. Layers 1-5: draw from facts rated "easy" (high fun_score, low difficulty). Layers 6-10: mix of easy and medium. Layers 11-15: medium and hard. Layers 16-20: hard only, with bonus rewards for correct answers. This creates a natural difficulty progression that mirrors the mining difficulty. A player who reaches layer 15 should feel tested both mechanically (hard blocks, dangerous hazards) and intellectually (harder quiz questions). It also prevents the cognitive dissonance of answering a trivial question while surrounded by deadly lava.

### Q43: How should the "quiz for artifact pickup" system feel different from quiz gates?
**Context**: DD-V2-026 says quizzes should trigger on artifact pickup (scaling with rarity: Common 20%, Rare 50%, Epic 75%, Legendary 100%). Quiz gates block progression until answered. These are fundamentally different interactions but currently use the same overlay.
**My Advice**: Differentiate them visually and mechanically. Quiz gates: full-screen overlay with dramatic "barrier dissolving" animation on correct answer, heavy O2 penalty on wrong. Artifact quizzes: smaller, less intrusive "GAIA scan" overlay — "Let me analyze this artifact..." with a quiz. Correct answer boosts rarity (existing mechanic). Wrong answer: artifact is still collected but at base rarity with no boost opportunity. This makes artifact quizzes feel like bonus opportunities rather than punishments, preserving the excitement of finding an artifact rather than gating it behind anxiety.

### Q44: Should the pop quiz system have a "streak bonus" for consecutive correct answers in a single dive?
**Context**: `RANDOM_QUIZ_CHANCE: 0.04` (planned increase to 8-10%). Reward is a flat 8 dust per correct answer, 5 O2 penalty for wrong. There is no in-run streak tracking.
**My Advice**: Add an in-run quiz streak multiplier: 1st correct = 8 dust, 2nd consecutive = 12 dust (1.5x), 3rd = 16 dust (2x), 4th+ = 24 dust (3x, capped). A wrong answer resets the multiplier to 1x. Display the current multiplier prominently ("Quiz Streak: 3x"). This creates a micro-game within the dive — players start caring about maintaining their quiz streak, which increases learning engagement (they pay more attention to avoid breaking the streak). The dust reward at 3x is generous enough to meaningfully supplement income but not so much that it outpaces mining.

### Q45: How should the consistency penalty (`CONSISTENCY_PENALTY_O2: 8`) feel to the player?
**Context**: Getting a previously-mastered fact wrong costs an extra 8 O2 on top of the normal wrong-answer penalty. An orange warning appears. The SM-2 algorithm requires 2+ successful reps before the penalty applies.
**My Advice**: 8 O2 extra is too subtle to be felt as a distinct mechanic — it blends into the normal wrong-answer cost. Either make it dramatically noticeable (20 O2 penalty + screen flash + GAIA disappointed reaction: "You knew this one! What happened?") or remove it entirely. The middle ground is the worst option — punishing enough to feel bad but not distinct enough to feel like a designed system. If you keep it, add a visual "broken chain" icon to show the mastery regression, making the consequence visible and motivating the player to re-master the fact.

### Q46: What should the target ratio of mining time to quiz time be within a single dive?
**Context**: With the planned 8-10% pop quiz rate, plus quiz gates per layer, plus artifact/oxygen/crate quizzes, a 5-minute dive could involve 8-15 quizzes. If each quiz takes 10-15 seconds (read question, consider options, answer), that is 2-4 minutes of quiz time out of 5 minutes total. This would make the game feel more like a quiz app with mining breaks than a mining game with quiz integration.
**My Advice**: Target 70% mining, 30% quiz for a standard dive. That means ~3.5 minutes mining, ~1.5 minutes quiz in a 5-minute session. Cap quiz frequency at roughly 1 quiz per 30 seconds of active play, regardless of trigger sources. If a player hits a pop quiz trigger within 15 seconds of their last quiz, skip it silently. The quiz rate should also adapt to player behavior — if a player is clearly rushing (mining 3+ blocks/second), reduce quiz rate to avoid breaking flow state. If a player is exploring methodically, maintain the full rate.

### Q47: Should there be non-quiz knowledge moments during mining?
**Context**: All knowledge delivery currently happens through quizzes (4-choice MCQ). The design doc mentions ambient storytelling ("environmental flavor text") but this is atmospheric, not educational. Data Discs deliver facts but are rare (1 per mine).
**My Advice**: Add "Fact Fragments" — when mining certain blocks, a one-line fact appears briefly at the bottom of the screen (2 seconds, no interaction required). "Diamonds form at ~150 km depth under extreme pressure." These are not quizzed on in that moment — they seed knowledge that might appear in a quiz later. Players subconsciously absorb facts while mining. This is the "environmental learning" layer that makes the mine feel educational without interrupting flow. Show 1-2 fact fragments per layer, tied to the biome (volcanic biome yields geology facts, fossilized biome yields paleontology facts). Track which facts were shown as fragments — if that fact appears in a quiz later, display "You saw this earlier!" for a confidence boost.

### Q48: How should screen shake, particles, and juice scale with mobile performance?
**Context**: DD-V2-015B mandates "particle effects everywhere" and DD-V2-012B requires screen shake on every hit. The primary platform is mobile (Capacitor on Android first). Budget Android devices may struggle with heavy particle effects + canvas rendering + Phaser 3.
**My Advice**: Implement a 3-tier visual effects setting: Full (all particles, all shake, all animations — for modern devices), Reduced (50% particle count, no ambient particles, minimal shake — for mid-range), Minimal (no particles, no shake, simple block-break flash — for budget devices). Default to "Reduced" and let players upgrade to "Full" if performance allows. Auto-detect by measuring frame time during the first 10 seconds of a dive — if average is above 20ms (below 50 FPS), suggest switching to Reduced. Never sacrifice responsiveness for visual flair — a smooth 60 FPS with no particles is always better than a juicy 30 FPS.

### Q49: Should daily streaks reward consistency or intensity?
**Context**: The streak system rewards consecutive days (3-day through 100-day milestones). Streak freezes cost 200 dust (max 3). The design focuses entirely on daily check-in frequency with no consideration of session depth or quality.
**My Advice**: Complement the daily streak with a "dive depth" streak — consecutive dives where the player reaches a deeper layer than their previous best (or matches it). "You reached layer 5 three dives in a row!" with escalating rewards. This rewards improving skill, not just opening the app. The daily streak handles retention (come back every day). The depth streak handles engagement quality (actually push yourself each session). Display both streaks in the HUD: "Day 7 / Depth 3" — players who excel at both feel doubly rewarded. Players who only check in briefly still keep their daily streak, reducing frustration.

### Q50: How should the game handle the transition from "learning new facts" to "reviewing mastered facts"?
**Context**: The SM-2 spaced repetition system creates a growing pool of "due for review" facts. As a player masters more facts, their review queue grows. At 500+ learned facts, morning/evening review rituals (5 cards each) will barely dent the queue. The mine quiz system draws from both new and review pools. Eventually, reviews could crowd out new content.
**My Advice**: This is the most critical long-term design question. Cap the "due for review" queue presented to the player at 20 facts per day (10 morning, 10 evening). If more are due, prioritize by SM-2 urgency (longest overdue first). For in-mine quizzes, use a 60/40 split: 60% new or recently-seen facts, 40% due reviews. Never let reviews exceed 50% of in-mine quiz encounters — the mine should always feel like discovery, not homework. For facts that are "well mastered" (5+ successful reviews, ease > 3.0), move them to a "deep archive" that only surfaces once every 90 days. This prevents the review queue from growing unboundedly while still reinforcing long-term retention. The player should feel like they are always discovering, with review woven in seamlessly — never like they are drowning in flashcards.
